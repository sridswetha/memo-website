## Historical Context
Who are public restrooms actually designed for? While they appear neutral, these spaces are built around an assumed “normal” body, quietly excluding anyone who does not fit that standard. Institutions are built around an idealized, unmarked “normate” body, treating departures as problems to be managed privately. Transgender and non-binary people using sex-segregated spaces face this dynamic: their bodies are marked as departures from an assumed norm, and public infrastructure reflects that exclusion. All-gender bathrooms address what Garland-Thomson calls the "ability/disability system.” Bates College reports, “Historically, restrooms have been a place for harassment, discrimination and threats of injury and arrest towards transgender people. All-gender restrooms provide an opportunity for our community members to enter a room without being questioned or interrogated.” 

## Cultural Context
In 2021, UMD put pad/tampon dispensers in women’s restrooms to end period poverty and prevent menstruation from interrupting education. “If we thought about all bodies potentially menstruating and needing menstrual products, then those products would be available just like toilet paper is available,” Mathiason said. The department also identified, “103 public locations in dorms to put the menstrual products, including all-gender and women’s restrooms on first floors and in multipurpose rooms.” UMD standards are meant to check and restock menstrual products daily during cleaning times to ensure availability. 

Gender-neutral bathrooms still see men as the baseline. Some gender-neutral bathrooms offer products, others do not, which aligns with Sandra Harding’s standpoint theory: the absence of menstrual products in all-gender bathrooms reveals structural bias. Harding says, “Will not the selection and definition of problems always bear the social fingerprints of the dominant groups in a culture.” Similarly, Susan Bordo's "docile body" concept shows that people are culturally disciplined to keep menstruation private, so public infrastructure without products feels normal instead of exclusionary.

## Necessity for Intervention
Menstrual products offered in women's bathrooms on the UMD campus are not consistently available in all-gender restrooms. Garland-Thomson notes this legitimates “an unequal distribution of resources, status, and power within a biased social and architectural environment." Without menstrual products, transgender and non-binary individuals lack equitable access. Frank (2020) found that bathrooms are a key site of contested gender identity for trans and non-binary menstruators. Missing products signal their bodies are unwelcome, forcing use of binary bathrooms and undermining all-gender restroom purposes. All users should benefit from period equity.

## Bathroom Audit
To gain firsthand insight, we visited 32 main on-campus buildings to audit all-gender bathrooms and menstrual product availability, reflecting Harding’s standpoint methodology. The audit targeted buildings accessible to students. Data was compiled into an Excel spreadsheet.

We cross-referenced the campus map with building audits. Some family restrooms were actually all-gender (Mckledin, Benjamin Building). Out of 451 campus buildings, only 64 were listed as having all-gender bathrooms. Of the 32 buildings audited, 25 had at least one all-gender bathroom; only 11 contained menstrual products. Some bathrooms labeled as all-gender were misidentified or inaccessible (½ at Knight Hall, Woods Hall; Jimenez).

We hosted a booth outside Yahentimitsi Dining Hall from 11:30–1:30, sharing that only 34% of buildings had menstrual products in all-gender bathrooms. Participants answered questions on sticky notes:

- “How much of a responsibility does UMD have to provide pads/tampons in all-gender bathrooms?”
- “Would you use an all-gender bathroom? Why or why not?”
- “Would you feel comfortable carrying a pad in public? Why or why not?”

Responses addressed administrative responsibility, stigma, and privacy, informed by Bordo and trans/non-binary menstruators’ experiences.

## Interpretation of Responses
- Carrying pads/tampons: Most normalize menstruation; some feel stigma if concealment is difficult.
- Responsibility for menstrual products: Majority believe UMD is responsible; products seen as infrastructure.
- Use of all-gender bathrooms: Majority comfortable; privacy is key; some discomfort remains if products missing.

## Success of the Event
The event collected 56 responses in 2 hours, generating quantitative (audit) and qualitative (opinions) data. Interactive elements allowed participants to see other perspectives while engaging.

## Improvement
Future events could include disability-focused questions and demographic data to analyze correlations. Booth timing could vary to reach more students.

## What We Learned
Students overwhelmingly support menstrual equity in all-gender bathrooms. Issues are about visibility, inclusivity, and prioritization of needs. Bordo shows shame around menstruation is socially produced; Harding highlights structural bias. By centering marginalized experiences, our project acts as standpoint-based knowledge production.
